I remember starting to like technology stuff when I was around five years old when I got my first phone. I think it was a Nokia 5110, handed down to me by my aunt sometime around 2003 or 2004. It is still one of my clearest childhood memories as I played around with the phone, tried to make something cool even though I didn't know much about it, and, of course, played Snake.

I also remember getting an IBM ThinkPad 600 series around that period. If I remember correctly, it had a Pentium II processor, around 128 MB of RAM, and perhaps a 2 GB hard drive and also it was running Windows 98. My uncle gave me pirated copies of _Virtua Fighter_ and _Sega Rally_, and it was such a joy to play them. Another thing that I also like to do spent hours being creative with WordArt and trying to write things in Microsoft Word. At that time, technology felt like something I could explore. I didn't need to do anything productive with it. I just enjoyed finding out what it could do.

I grew up during the transition from devices that were mostly offline to technology that expects us to be connected all the time. By middle school, I was already chronically online. Even on ordinary days, my screen time now often exceeds four hours. During a period when I was depressed, it sometimes reached close to ten hours. At that point, I was not necessarily enjoying most of the time I spent online. Moving between applications had simply become normal for me.

Maybe you have experienced something similar?

My interest in older devices did not come from lacking modern ones. Until 2024, I mostly used my newer phone, laptop, and desktop computer. I also have a gaming desktop with a relatively recent GPU. Choosing to use older devices came after years of using modern hardware.

I did not originally start looking for old technology as a solution to my screen time. I was fascinated by how advanced some devices had been when they were released, and by the fact that many of them were still alive and useful more than a decade later. Becoming more intentional about technology was something I discovered afterward.

## Keeping Old Technology Alive

In the winter of 2025, I bought a used Nokia 808 PureView. This was not a phone I discovered by accident. I still remember first learning about it from a phone magazine in 2012. The idea that a phone could have a 41-megapixel camera at that time was amazing to me. I was fascinated by how advanced it seemed, even though I could not own one back then.

Years later, after moving to Finland, I finally managed to snipe one at a reasonable price on Tori. Using it gave me some of the same excitement I imagined I would have felt in 2012. The pictures were sharp, but they also still felt alive. I was amazed that a phone this old could produce pictures that looked this good.

I started taking a lot of pictures with it. Through that, I learned more about photo composition and began to understand what worked and what did not work when taking a picture.

I also installed custom firmware and spent time learning more about the phone. The Nokia 808 made me appreciate older technology a little more. More importantly, using it allowed me to be more present in my surroundings. Instead of opening another application and scrolling, I could take the phone out to photograph something I had noticed around me.

Another old device that has become a regular part of my life is my Nexus 6. I bought it in 2015, and I am still using it today. However, keeping it usable has not been effortless.

The Nexus 6 struggles to play some 2K videos. Without optimization, its battery only lasts around three to four hours on the original software. Many newer applications are also no longer compatible with that software. Modern software cannot completely remove the limitations of hardware from 2014.

However, I did more than simply accept those limitations. I repaired the phone, replaced its battery and mid-frame, and even made a video about the process. I then installed a LineageOS 21 custom build. This improved compatibility with newer applications and gave the phone a much more modern interface, including full gesture navigation instead of the old navigation buttons.

The interface works particularly well with the Nexus 6's 1440p AMOLED display. That screen is not only an impressive specification from its time. It still feels genuinely good when I read manga, articles, and other text. Gesture navigation also allows more of the large display to be used for the actual content.

I use another optimization tool that automatically enables airplane mode when the screen is off. The phone reconnects when I wake the screen again. With this setup, it stays offline when I am not using it but can still connect when I need it. It can last around two or three days without charging, which is pretty good for a phone this old.

I mostly use the Nexus 6 as a Wi-Fi tablet. When I go outside, I can keep it offline. When I get home, I synchronize it with newer manga chapters, music, and news. It is slow in some situations, but for reading and listening to music, it still does what I need.

The other device I regularly use is the Panasonic Lumix LX2. It is an old camera, but it is compact, and the pictures still look very nice if I only want to share them online or print them at a reasonable size. I don't need the huge resolutions offered by newer phones and cameras for that.

The Lumix LX2 can also capture RAW images, which is impressive for a camera from that period. However, after taking one RAW photo, it needs around five seconds to save the file. During those five seconds, I cannot take another picture. This means I have to think more carefully about when I press the shutter. Sometimes that makes me more deliberate, but it is still a real limitation. If the moment changes quickly, the camera may simply be too slow.

What I dislike about many modern smartphone photos is not simply that they are processed. To my eyes, the processing can make everything feel strangely flat. At the same time, some details look excessively sharp while other parts look too smooth. People and other subjects can sometimes appear separated from the rest of the image instead of naturally belonging to the scene.

The Lumix LX2 does not always produce a technically better image, especially in difficult lighting. However, I often find its photos more natural and alive. The camera leaves more imperfections in the image, and I think those imperfections are part of what I enjoy.

These are not the only older devices I still use. I still code on my eight-year-old laptop, and I still write and watch things on my five-year-old iPad Air. I don't use them as much as the Nexus 6 or the Lumix LX2, but they still work for what I need.

## The Limitations I Was Looking For

Using these devices has changed what I expect from technology, but that does not make every limitation a hidden benefit. Waiting five seconds for the Lumix LX2 to save a RAW file can make me miss another picture, and custom firmware doesnt make the Nexus 6 run the all apps smoothly, they still pretty much too old for modern applications. Keeping these devices useful requires repairs, software modifications, optimization, and some changes in how I use them. Still, if I understand those limitations, a device does not need to do everything. It only needs to do the particular job I chose it for.

I did not start buying old devices to escape modern technology. I was originally fascinated by what these devices could do and why they were still alive. But as the hobby grew, it also became connected to my exhaustion with always-online technology.

That creates a contradiction I cannot completely ignore. These devices may have helped me use technology more intentionally, but in the process, I also bought more things that I probably did not need.

Maybe I have become more intentional about how I use technology, but I am not sure that I have become less consumerist. I may have only replaced the excitement of buying new technology with the excitement of discovering old technology.

For now, I don't have a clean answer to that. I only know that these old devices have made me think more carefully about what I actually need from technology, and why I keep wanting more of it.
